Fabrication, Re-Use of Work & Plagiarism

Fabrication

Fabrication is:

"The intentional use of invented information or the falsification of research or other findings."

The use of false information in academic tasks is a serious allegation. In your studies here as part of the UNF academic community, it's important that you maintain good research practices and use only reliable and credible research sources. If you are unsure how to find or determine the credibility of a source, please visit the Library for research assistance.

For Example

Some examples of fabrication include:

  • Inventing data or a source of information,

  • Listing sources that you did not actually use in a Reference List or Bibliography,

  • Submitting someone else's work as your own.

Re-Use of Work

Re-use of work is also a problem from an academic integrity perspective. Re-use of work is defined by the University of Niagara Falls Canada as:

"Submitting one's own work for credit in more than one course without the permission of faculty,

or re-submitting work, in whole or in part, for which credit has already been granted".

This one often comes as a surprise to students; it's my own work, so why can't I use it?

Submitting work that was already completed and submitted for credit for another class or purpose constitutes academic misconduct because it is dishonest behaviour. You are missing out on the learning that each academic task offers if you are taking shortcuts to complete them. If you have some research or previously completed work that you think may contribute valuably to another assignment, discuss it with your professor and describe exactly how you would use the previously completed work in your current assignment.

Ensure that you have full permission from your instructor before re-using your own work.

For Example

Some examples of re-use of work include:

  • Re-submitting an assignment that you completed and already submitted for another class,

  • Re-using research compilations that you completed to meet the learning objectives for another class without first obtaining permission from your current professor,

  • Re-using digital content developed in another class for credit without first obtaining permission from your current professor.

Plagiarism

The University of Niagara Falls Canada defines plagiarism as:

"The use of ideas, words, images, phrases, or digital content belonging to or produced by

another and claiming it as one's own instead of using proper referencing procedures."

When a student submits work for academic credit and includes their name on it, the student is certifying that the work is their own original submission unless otherwise acknowledged and attributed. This is the purpose of referencing and citations; you are permitted to use outside sources to supplement your own learning, as long as you properly acknowledge that the work is not your own and credit the original author. If you do not, you may fall under accusations of plagiarism.

For Example

Some examples of plagiarism include:

  • Submitting and/or representing, copying, paraphrasing, or summarizing someone else's words, information, analyses, or other content as your own,

  • Neglecting to properly use citations in your work.
